⚡ Reference
Ohm's Law
V = I × RVoltage = Current × Resistance. The foundation of all circuit math.
I = V ÷ RCurrent = Voltage ÷ Resistance. Find amps when you know volts and ohms.
R = V ÷ IResistance = Voltage ÷ Current. Find ohms when you know volts and amps.
Power (Watt's Law)
P = V × IPower = Voltage × Current. Most common power formula.
P = I² × RUse when you have current and resistance but no voltage.
P = V² ÷ RUse when you have voltage and resistance but no current.
I = P ÷ VFind current from power rating and voltage.
R = V² ÷ PFind resistance from voltage and power.
Series Circuits
R_T = R₁+R₂+R₃Total resistance = sum of all resistors. Current is the same everywhere.
V_T = V₁+V₂+V₃Voltage drops add up to equal the source voltage (KVL).
Parallel Circuits
1/R_T=1/R₁+1/R₂General parallel resistance formula. R_T is always less than smallest branch.
R_T=(R₁×R₂)÷(R₁+R₂)Shortcut for exactly TWO parallel resistors.
R_T = R ÷ nWhen all n resistors are equal value R.
I_T = I₁+I₂+I₃Branch currents add up (KCL). Voltage is same across all branches.
Kirchhoff's Laws
ΣI_in = ΣI_outKCL: Current into a node = current out. Conservation of charge.
ΣV = 0 (loop)KVL: Sum of all voltages around a closed loop = zero.
AC & Transformers
V₁/V₂ = N₁/N₂Transformer voltage ratio = turns ratio.
Vrms = Vp ÷ √2RMS = Peak × 0.707. The effective (working) AC voltage.
Vp = Vrms × 1.414Peak voltage from RMS. 120V outlet peaks at ~170V.
T = 1 ÷ fPeriod (seconds) = 1 ÷ frequency. At 60Hz: T = 0.0167s.
XL = 2πfLInductive reactance in ohms. Increases with frequency.
Wire Sizing (AWG)
14 AWG → 15AMinimum for 15A circuits. General lighting.
12 AWG → 20AKitchen, bathroom, workshop outlets.
10 AWG → 30ADryers, A/C units, water heaters.
8 AWG → 40AElectric ranges, large A/C.
Lower # = ThickerLower AWG number = thicker wire = more current capacity.
Derating Rules
4–6 wires → 80%Derate ampacity to 80% when 4–6 conductors in same conduit.
7–9 wires → 70%Derate to 70% for 7–9 conductors bundled together.
10–20 wires → 50%Derate to 50% for 10–20 conductors.

🔬 Meters & Instruments
Voltmeter
Measures voltage between two points. Always connected in parallel (across) the component. Has very high internal resistance so it doesn't affect the circuit.
Ammeter
Measures current flow. Always connected in series — all current must pass through it. Near-zero internal resistance. NEVER connect in parallel — will short-circuit and destroy the meter.
Clamp-on Ammeter
Measures current via the magnetic field around one conductor. No circuit interruption needed. Clamp around ONE wire only — clamping both cancels the reading.
Ohmmeter / DMM
Measures resistance. Circuit MUST be de-energized first. OL or ∞ = open circuit (broken). 0Ω = short or closed switch. External voltage will damage the meter.
Megohmmeter (Megger)
Tests insulation resistance by applying 500–2,500V. Reads in megohms (MΩ). Below 1 MΩ = failed insulation. Always ground the cable after testing — stored charge can shock you.
Tachometer
Measures rotational speed directly in RPM. Contact type touches the shaft end; optical type uses a reflective strip without contact. Used to verify a motor runs at its rated speed.
Hydrometer
Measures specific gravity of battery electrolyte. Fully charged: ~1.265. Discharged: ~1.120. The definitive tool for checking a lead-acid battery's state of charge.
Wattmeter
Measures electrical power in watts. Connects with both a voltage coil (in parallel) and a current coil (in series). Used on 3-phase circuits to measure total power draw.
Oscilloscope
Displays electrical waveforms — voltage plotted over time. Used to observe AC wave shape, frequency, and peak voltage. Essential for troubleshooting electronic circuits.
Non-Contact Tester
Detects AC voltage presence without touching conductors — senses the electromagnetic field. Quick first safety check only; verify with a meter for actual readings.
🔧 Tools
Fish Tape
Steel or fiberglass tape threaded through conduit to pull wire. Use fiberglass in energized conduit — steel conducts electricity. Use wire pulling lubricant on long runs.
Conduit Bender
Bends EMT, IMC, or rigid conduit. Match the bender shoe size exactly to the conduit size. Angle markings on the bender guide 90°, 45°, and back-to-back bends.
Knockout Punch
Creates clean circular holes in metal panels and enclosures. Uses hydraulic or threaded force to punch through without distorting surrounding metal. Size must match conduit diameter.
Hydraulic Crimper
Permanently connects cable lugs to large conductors. Required for power cable terminations — wire nuts are only for smaller conductors. Creates a gas-tight, high-current connection.
Wire Stripper
Removes insulation without nicking the conductor. Each notch is sized for a specific gauge. Match the notch to the wire gauge — a wrong size will nick or fail to cut cleanly.
Lineman's Pliers
The all-purpose heavy-duty electrical hand tool. Cuts wire, twists wire nut connections, grips conductors. Also called side-cutting pliers. Standard in every electrician's belt.
Torque Screwdriver
Tightens terminals to a manufacturer-specified torque value. Prevents loose connections (fire hazard) and over-tightening (cracks terminals). Required by NEC for breaker terminals.
Fuse Puller
Safely removes cartridge fuses from energized fuse clips without hand contact. Keeps fingers away from exposed live metal. Never remove a live cartridge fuse by hand.
Reamer
Removes the sharp internal burr left after cutting conduit. The burr can cut through wire insulation during pulling. Always ream before pulling any conductors — NEC required.
Knockout Punch
Creates clean circular holes in metal enclosures for conduit entry. Size-specific — must match conduit trade size. Cleaner result than a hole saw on sheet metal.
⚙️ Motor Terms
FLA
Full Load Amps — the current the motor draws when running at its rated horsepower. The key nameplate value for sizing conductors, overload relays, and breakers.
Service Factor (SF)
Overload capacity multiplier. SF 1.15 means the motor can run continuously at 115% of rated HP without damage. Built-in headroom for occasional extra demand.
Insulation Class
Temperature rating of motor windings. Class A = 105°C, B = 130°C, F = 155°C, H = 180°C. Higher class = more heat resistant. Match to the motor's operating environment.
Overload Relay
Protects motor windings from sustained overcurrent and heat. Trips before windings burn out. Separate from the circuit breaker, which protects the wiring. Both are required.
Commutator
The rotating switching device in DC motors that transfers current to the armature windings via carbon brushes. Inspect for discoloration, uneven wear, and sparking during operation.
Carbon Brushes
Stationary carbon contacts that ride on the commutator and carry current to the rotating armature. Should exert ~1.5 lbs/sq inch pressure. Worn or loose brushes cause sparking.
Squirrel-Cage Motor
The most common AC induction motor. Has no commutator, slip rings, or brushes — current is induced magnetically into the rotor. Rugged, low maintenance, widely used in industry.
Inrush Current
The starting current surge, typically 6–8× the FLA, lasting a fraction of a second. The reason motor circuits use time-delay fuses and breakers — standard types would trip on startup.
Shunt Winding
DC motor field winding connected in parallel with the armature. Many turns of fine wire = high resistance. Limits its own current. A shorted shunt winding causes dangerous overspeed.
Series Winding
DC motor field winding in series with the armature. Few turns of heavy wire = low resistance. Carries full armature current. Provides high starting torque. Used in compound motors.
NEMA Starter
A motor controller combining a contactor (the switch) and an overload relay (the protection) in one unit. Sized by NEMA rating (0–9) based on motor HP and voltage.
Synchronous Speed
The theoretical speed of an AC motor's rotating magnetic field: 120 × frequency ÷ number of poles. A 4-pole motor at 60Hz has a sync speed of 1,800 RPM.
